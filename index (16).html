Smart Garage Predictive
Maintenance

Catch failures before they happen. Keep customers safe, satisfied, and subscribed.

Problem

Garage doors fail without warning — and that's a trust problem.

A garage door is one of the most frequently used mechanical systems in a home, cycling hundreds of times each year. Motor brushes degrade. Sensors drift out of alignment. Springs weaken under load. Yet most smart garage platforms treat the hardware like a dumb relay — open or closed — and only respond once something breaks. Customers experience expensive service calls, security gaps, and frustration directed squarely at the brand. The first company to get ahead of that moment wins loyalty and recurring revenue.

Product Vision

Turn sensor data into a health score that works on behalf of the customer.

Every actuation of a myQ-connected garage door generates signals: motor current draw, vibration signature, open/close duration, and sensor confirmation latency. Baseline those signals per unit and over a fleet of millions, and anomalies become meaningful early warnings. The product vision is an intelligent maintenance layer living inside the existing mobile app — no new hardware required — that surfaces a device health score, explains what's degrading in plain language, and connects users to a vetted service partner before failure ever happens.

Key Features

What we build

  • Vibration and motor current anomaly detection using on-device baseline models, updated over the air as the fleet grows.
  • Component failure probability score shown as a simple health dial — not a wall of telemetry data — in the main app home screen.
  • Contextual push notifications triggered at meaningful thresholds: "Your motor is showing early wear. Schedule a check before summer heat peaks."
  • In-app service marketplace with vetted technicians, real-time availability, and transparent pricing. Revenue share unlocks a new stream.
  • Maintenance history log so homeowners have documentation when they sell the property — a real, underserved pain point.

Monetization · Revenue Strategy · Go-to-Market

Smart Access Pricing
& Bundling Strategy

Design a product and pricing portfolio that captures value at every willingness-to-pay tier.

Business Problem

One-time hardware revenue doesn't build a durable business.

Smart home hardware margins are brutal — commoditized components, intense retail competition, and consumers who compare on price alone. The companies winning in this space have figured out that the hardware is the acquisition channel, not the profit center. Revenue and margin come from recurring subscriptions, premium add-ons, and enterprise contracts. The opportunity is to design a bundle architecture that meets customers where they are at purchase, then pulls them up the value ladder through genuine product quality — not dark patterns.

Strategic Approach

Anchor low, differentiate high, lock in with enterprise.

The pricing strategy runs three parallel tracks: a competitive entry-level hardware price that drives volume and activations; a mid-tier hardware bundle that bundles camera and alerts to lift average order value; and a monthly subscription tier that monetizes power users through AI automation and insights. Enterprise fleet management sits above all of it on a custom contract model, targeting property managers, dealerships, and logistics companies who manage dozens or hundreds of access points. Each tier is designed to feel complete — not artificially hobbled to force upgrades.

Revenue Model Logic

How each tier contributes to margin

  • Basic ($199): Hardware at modest margin. Goal is activation volume and ecosystem entry. Funds future upsell attempts via push and in-app surface.
  • Secure Home ($299): Camera accessory has ~55% gross margin. Bundle ASP lift of $100 with minimal incremental COGS. Strong retail story at Target and Costco.
  • Pro Automation ($12/mo): SaaS-model recurring revenue. Near-100% gross margin on software features. Target 25% of activated hardware base on subscription within 18 months.
  • Enterprise (Custom): Land and expand with a pilot deployment. Annual contracts. NRR target 115%+ as customers add access points and admin seats.
STRATEGY 01

Competitive Benchmarking

Map Chamberlain, Ring, and Arlo pricing across tiers. Identify where we're overpriced (hardware), underpriced (subscription), and differentiated (AI maintenance). Prioritize subscription price integrity over hardware race-to-bottom. Avoid the trap of discounting hardware to drive activation — that's a margin killer with no floor.

STRATEGY 02

A/B Pricing Experiment Plan

Run a structured experiment across three variables: subscription price point ($8 vs. $12 vs. $16/mo), trial length (7-day vs. 30-day), and feature gating for the health score. Measure conversion rate, retention at 90 days, and net revenue impact. Use Bayesian stopping rules so we don't wait 12 weeks for a signal — decisions in 3 to 4 weeks.

STRATEGY 03

Go-to-Market Sequencing

Launch Basic and Secure Home at retail first — drive hardware volume and app activations. Introduce Pro Automation subscription to the activated base six months post-launch, when users have experienced the product value. Enterprise motion runs in parallel through direct sales, targeting property management companies where the fleet dashboard has clearest ROI.

UX Design · Platform Strategy · Ecosystem Thinking

Unified Smart Access
Dashboard

One interface. Every door, gate, camera, and guest — fully under control.

UX Problem

Three devices, three apps, zero coherence.

A typical connected home customer has a myQ garage opener, a Ring camera on the front door, a gate controller from a third vendor, and a lockbox for package delivery. Managing access across all of them means context-switching between apps, no unified activity log, and no single place to grant guest access across the full perimeter. Every additional device added to the ecosystem should make the product more valuable — but right now, more devices means more friction. That's a design failure with serious churn implications.

Platform Vision

A control layer that scales with the home, not against it.

The unified dashboard isn't just a redesigned home screen. It's a platform decision — one that requires engineering investment in a device abstraction layer, partnerships or integrations with third-party hardware, and a UX system that can accommodate new device categories without constant redesign. Done well, it becomes the reason customers buy their second, third, and fourth devices from us rather than from Amazon or Google. The strategic moat is the control surface, not any single piece of hardware.

Feature Set

What makes this more than a list of devices

  • Single control panel showing status, health score, and last event for every connected access point — garage, gate, camera, smart lock, delivery lockbox.
  • Real-time activity timeline that merges events across all devices into one chronological feed. Filter by device, user, or event type.
  • Guest access management with role-based permissions — full access, time-limited window, or single-use. Works across all devices from one screen.
  • Delivery access permissions that integrate with FedEx and UPS to auto-open a window during delivery, then close and notify.
  • Usage analytics showing weekly and monthly patterns — most active times, most frequent users, anomalous access events — exportable for security review.
Roadmap Prioritization

How to sequence a platform build

  • Phase 1 (0–3 months): Unified device list and real-time status for first-party myQ hardware only. Establishes the design system and data model. Low engineering risk, high visual impact for users already on platform.
  • Phase 2 (3–6 months): Activity log merge and guest access unification across devices. This is the feature with highest engagement impact — power users currently manage this manually.
  • Phase 3 (6–12 months): Third-party device integrations via Works with myQ program. API published for partners. Ring, Yale, and Schlage are first-tier targets based on install base overlap.
  • Phase 4 (12–18 months): Delivery access automation with carrier APIs. Analytics dashboard and data export for Pro and Enterprise tiers.
Stakeholder Alignment Plan

Getting hardware, firmware, and product moving together

A unified dashboard sounds like a design project. It isn't. It requires firmware changes to expose richer telemetry, a platform API that hardware partners can certify against, mobile engineering capacity, and a data infrastructure team to handle the event-merge pipeline at scale. Getting those four groups aligned on a single roadmap requires a few specific moves:

  • Monthly joint planning session with firmware and mobile leads, not just emails. Block the time at the start of each quarter.
  • A shared success metric that all teams own: device attach rate per customer. Ties hardware, software, and partnerships to one number.
  • A public internal roadmap so firmware teams know what mobile needs six months ahead — no last-minute API surprises in either direction.
